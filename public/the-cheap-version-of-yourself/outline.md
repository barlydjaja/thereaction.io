   you. - A bug you "solved" without understanding. - An essay you didn't write

   the thesis. It should make the reader think huh, that's true — and then keep
   reading to find out why it matters.

2. Name what AI actually gives you. Not
   speed. Not knowledge. A finished output without the becoming. The thing you
   would have eventually produced, handed to you before you changed in the process

4. The real argument.
   Selfhood is a residue. You are what's left after you've struggled with things. Writing makes you a clearer thinker not because writing is magic, but because the parts of
   you that can't think clearly get sanded off in the act. Same for code, for arguments, for relationships you have to repair by hand.

AI removes the sanding.

5. The drift.
   You don't notice it the first time. Or the tenth. But the cheap version of you ships faster, talks smoother, looks more competent. People respond to it. You respond to
   it. Eventually you start outsourcing the parts of yourself you used to be proud of, because the replacement is good enough and the cost is invisible.

The frightening part: you can't tell from inside whether it's happening.

6. Pre-empt the obvious objection.
   Isn't this just every tool? Calculators, spellcheckers, Google?
   No — and you have to earn this. Previous tools removed labor. AI removes the practice of thought itself: the having of the idea, the wrestling with the wrong version,
   the moment you realize what you actually believe. That's not labor. That's where you come from.

7. A test.
   Give the reader something to check against. Something like:
   If an AI could do this for you and you'd be content with the result, you're probably not doing it for the result.
   Find the things you don't want done for you. Those are the things making you.

8. Close larger than you started.
   Bring it back to the opening moment, but reframed. End on something quiet, not a manifesto. PG closings rarely tell you what to do — they leave you with a sentence you
   keep thinking about for a week.

A possible direction (don't use verbatim, write your own): The work was never the point. The work was the way you became someone who could do the work. If you give that
away, you're not faster — you're just absent.

Length target: ~1,200–1,800 words. Long enough to earn the argument, short enough that every paragraph has to justify itself.

One discipline while drafting: if a paragraph could be cut without losing the spine, cut it. PG essays feel inevitable because everything that wasn't load-bearing got
removed.
